A Life & A Nation Changed

Timeline

February 12, 1809
Born in a Log Cabin — Hardin County, Kentucky
Lincoln was born into poverty on the frontier. His family moved to Indiana when he was seven, and he largely educated himself through borrowed books. His mother, Nancy Hanks Lincoln, died when he was nine.
1831
Moves to New Salem, Illinois
At 22, Lincoln struck out on his own, working as a store clerk, postmaster, and land surveyor while voraciously reading law books. He became known locally for honesty and storytelling.
1836
Admitted to the Illinois Bar
Self-taught, Lincoln passed the bar and began a law practice in Springfield. He became one of Illinois' most respected lawyers, riding the judicial circuit across the state.
1842
Marries Mary Todd
After a famously turbulent courtship, Abraham married Mary Todd, a well-educated woman from a prominent Kentucky family. They had four sons together.
1847–1849
U.S. Congressman — Illinois
Lincoln served one term in the House of Representatives. He vocally opposed the Mexican-American War, which made him unpopular at home, and he did not seek re-election.
June 16, 1858
"A House Divided" Speech
Accepting the Republican nomination for Senate, Lincoln delivered his most famous pre-presidential speech, warning that the nation could not survive permanently half slave and half free. Though he lost the Senate race to Stephen Douglas, the Lincoln-Douglas debates made him a national figure.
Historic Speech
November 6, 1860
Elected 16th President of the United States
Lincoln won the presidency without carrying a single Southern state. Within weeks, South Carolina seceded. By February 1861, six more states had followed, forming the Confederate States of America.
President
April 12, 1861
Civil War Begins — Fort Sumter Attacked
Confederate forces opened fire on Union-held Fort Sumter in South Carolina, beginning the bloodiest war in American history. Lincoln called for 75,000 volunteers to suppress the rebellion. Over 620,000 soldiers would die before it ended.
Civil War
January 1, 1863
Emancipation Proclamation Takes Effect
Lincoln's executive order declared that all enslaved people in Confederate states "shall be then, thenceforward, and forever free." While it did not immediately free all slaves, it transformed the war's moral purpose and allowed Black men to enlist in the Union Army. Over 180,000 Black soldiers served.
Freedom
November 19, 1863
The Gettysburg Address
At the dedication of the Soldiers' National Cemetery in Gettysburg, Pennsylvania, Lincoln delivered a 272-word speech that reframed the Civil War as a fight not just for Union, but for the principle that all men are created equal. It is considered the greatest speech in American history.
Gettysburg
November 8, 1864
Re-elected President
Despite widespread war weariness, Lincoln won re-election over Democrat George McClellan. His victory ensured the war would continue until the Confederacy was defeated and slavery abolished — not negotiated away.
January 31, 1865
13th Amendment Passes Congress — Slavery Abolished
The House of Representatives passed the 13th Amendment to the Constitution, abolishing slavery throughout the United States. Lincoln had lobbied fiercely for its passage. It was ratified by the states on December 6, 1865 — months after his death. More than 4 million enslaved people were freed.
Slavery Abolished
April 9, 1865
Confederate General Lee Surrenders — Civil War Ends
General Robert E. Lee surrendered to Ulysses S. Grant at Appomattox Court House, Virginia. The war that had consumed four years and more than 620,000 lives was finally over. Lincoln was said to be visibly relieved and at peace.
Victory
April 14–15, 1865
Assassination at Ford's Theatre
Five days after Lee's surrender, Lincoln attended a performance of "Our American Cousin" at Ford's Theatre in Washington, D.C. Confederate sympathizer John Wilkes Booth shot him in the back of the head. Lincoln was carried to a boarding house across the street and died the following morning at 7:22 a.m. Secretary of War Edwin Stanton said: "Now he belongs to the ages."
Assassination

Why Honest Tom Aspires to Be Like Abe

The Standard

“
I am not bound to win, but I am bound to be true. I am not bound to succeed, but I am bound to live up to what light I have.
Abraham Lincoln

Honest Tom doesn't trade in half-truths. He doesn't dress up bad news. He calls the market like he sees it — when it's time to buy, he says so; when it's ugly, he says that too. Lincoln built a career on the same premise: that a man who tells the truth never has to remember what he said.

Lincoln came from nothing. No family money, no formal schooling, no connections. He read by firelight, taught himself law, and outworked everyone in the room. Tom sees that same work ethic as the only real edge in markets: preparation, discipline, and showing up every day when it would be easier not to.

And Lincoln understood something about the long game. He played it when everyone wanted a short one. He held the line on abolition when political advisors urged compromise. Honest Tom holds the line on conviction too — he doesn't panic-sell, doesn't chase noise, and doesn't let the crowd tell him what something is worth.

The portrait hangs as a reminder: be honest, be patient, do the work, and hold the line.
